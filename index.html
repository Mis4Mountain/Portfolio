The problem was not “meal planning.”

The deeper problem is household food visibility. People overbuy, forget items, and make last-minute food decisions because the kitchen has no memory layer.

User problem

Decision fatigue

Users stare at available food but still do not know what to cook. Kinji reframes the question from “what do I want?” to “what should I use first?”

Data problem

Invisible inventory

Food exists across fridge, freezer, pantry, receipts, habits, family preferences, and expiry windows. The product challenge is making that mess usable.

Business problem

Value must be visible

For subscription conversion, Kinji has to prove savings, not just generate recipes. The product needs a measurable value loop.

AI recipe system: from vague preference to structured output.

The work was not simply “use AI.” The product needed structured inputs, safety checks, household context, and cost-aware prompt logic.

Prompt expansion and recommendation pipeline

A practical AI workflow designed around inventory, preferences, allergies, and cost.

User intent

“I want something quick, warming, and not too heavy.”

Household context

Dietary restrictions, allergies, member preferences, cuisine direction, and role-based settings.

Pantry constraints

Available items, expiry risk, storage type, substitutions, and items to prioritize.

AI output

Recipe, substitutions, warnings, use-soon rationale, and estimated savings signal.

Safety example

Allergen warning, not hard blocking

The app can flag recipes that conflict with household restrictions while preserving user autonomy. This makes the product safer without making it rigid.

Trust example

Background processing indicators

Recipe generation and pantry parsing can take time. Processing states reduce confusion and reassure users that the app is working.

Household logic: the app needed permissions, not just profiles.

Shared kitchens create real product complexity: parents, partners, roommates, teenagers, allergies, preferences, and permissions.

Role 01

Owner

Controls household settings, members, preferences, and safety-critical defaults.

Role 02

Manager / Co-owner

A higher-permission role for partners or responsible family members who need shared control.

Role 03

Member

Can participate in the household while maintaining personal dietary needs and preferences.

The product decision I would test first.

I would test whether delaying mandatory household setup increases activation. The hypothesis: users should experience value first, then configure shared-household complexity once they trust the product.

ExperimentHypothesisPrimary metricDecision rule
Immediate household setup vs delayed setupDelaying household setup reduces onboarding abandonment.First recipe generated rateKeep delayed setup if first-recipe conversion rises without reducing later household adoption.
Recipe-first vs inventory-first onboardingA low-effort starter recipe increases perceived value.Time to First Useful MealKeep recipe-first if users return in Week 2 at a higher rate.
