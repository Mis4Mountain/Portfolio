Problem framing

The problem was not "meal planning."

The deeper problem is household food visibility. People overbuy, forget items, and make last-minute decisions because the kitchen has no memory layer. Kinji is a system that gives the kitchen a memory.

🤔
User problem

Decision fatigue

Users stare at available food but still do not know what to cook. Kinji reframes the question from "what do I want?" to "what should I use first?"

📦
Data problem

Invisible inventory

Food exists across fridge, freezer, pantry, receipts, habits, and expiry windows. The product challenge is making that invisible mess usable and actionable.

📈
Business problem

Value must be visible

For subscription conversion, Kinji has to prove savings, not just generate recipes. The product needs a measurable value loop that users can feel.

AI System

From vague preference to structured recipe output.

The work was not simply "use AI." The product needed structured inputs, safety checks, household context, and cost-aware prompt logic that could scale across a freemium tier model.

1

User intent

"Something quick, warming, and not too heavy tonight."

2

Household context

Dietary restrictions, allergies, member preferences, cuisine direction, and role-based settings.

3

Pantry constraints

Available items, expiry risk, storage type, substitutions, and items to prioritize by expiry.

4

AI structured output

Recipe, substitutions, allergen flags, use-soon rationale, and estimated savings signal.

Safety design

Allergen warning, not hard blocking

The app flags recipes that conflict with household restrictions while preserving user autonomy. This makes the product safer without making it rigid or paternalistic.

Cost thinking

AI unit economics per household

Prompt efficiency, inventory prioritization, and tiered usage limits are product decisions — not just engineering ones. I worked through token cost modeling to inform the pricing tier design.

KPI Definition Why it matters Product lever
Time to First Useful Meal Time from account creation to first recipe using at least one inventory item. Measures whether onboarding creates immediate payoff. Reduce required inputs; show starter pantry shortcuts.
Waste-Risk Conversion Share of expiring items used before estimated expiry. Direct measure of waste-reduction value proposition. Improve alerts, recipe ranking, and "use soon" visibility.
Household Collaboration Share of households adding at least one additional member or role. Signals whether Kinji becomes a shared household habit. Invite flow, role permissions, family-oriented UX.
AI Cost per Household API cost divided by active households across key usage events. Connects AI product usage to unit economics and tier design. Prompt efficiency, inventory prioritization, usage limits.

The product decision I would test first.

I would test whether delaying mandatory household setup increases activation. The hypothesis: users should experience value first, then configure shared complexity once they trust the product.

Experiment Hypothesis Primary metric Decision rule
Immediate vs delayed household setup Delaying household setup reduces onboarding abandonment without hurting later household adoption. First recipe generated rate Keep delayed setup if conversion rises without reducing household adoption in Week 4.
Recipe-first vs inventory-first onboarding A low-effort starter recipe increases perceived value and drives users to complete full setup afterward. Time to First Useful Meal Keep recipe-first if Week 2 return rate is higher with no significant drop in pantry depth.
Banner warnings vs modal interruptions Banner allergen warnings maintain autonomy while still improving safety awareness. Warning acknowledgement + recipe completion Use banners if completion is higher and warnings are still noticed and logged.
